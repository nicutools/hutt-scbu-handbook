SCBU – Introduction
Hutt Special Care Baby Unit has 12 beds, including 2 “rooming in” rooms 
It is a level 2 unit which means we do not routinely provide care for infants less than 32 weeks’ gestation at the time of delivery.
Mothers presenting in labour who are less than 32 weeks pregnant should be transferred to Wellington Hospital for delivery
Admission criteria:
Gestational age <35 weeks
Birth weight <2.3kg
Apnoeic or cyanotic episodes
Babies given naloxone at birth 
Respiratory distress
Suspected sepsis
Hypoglycaemia unable to be corrected with 2 doses of dextrose gel on the postnatal ward
Baby meeting admission criteria as per HIE protocol
Poor feeding requiring NGT
Significant birth defects or suspected surgical problems requiring SCBU level support
Babies requiring continuous monitoring and/or investigations for other medical indications e.g. convulsions, heart rhythm concerns etc. 
Babies transferred from NICU for ongoing care 
Babies are admitted under the care of the Consultant of the Week 
Babies born at Hutt and transferred to NICU continue under the care of the initial SMO when transferred back
Babies admitted for up to 4 hrs do not require formal admission or discharge from SCBU documentation but should have a note made in the Postnatal Discharge Summary 
Babies seen on Postnatal Ward but not admitted to SCBU:
If the child requires investigations (such as renal USS as IP or OP), create a virtual clinic on Concerto for the SCBU SMO so that appropriate labels are generated and the results are sent to the appropriate consultant
Please email the SMO when you are doing this so we have a record why the investigation is being done
Please make a note on the electronic post-natal discharge summary in the designated free text box
E.g. Baby had blood sugar monitoring for maternal gestational diabetes. Remained stable.  No follow-up needed” – this is a summary note, not a complete assessment documentation
This is vital so that GP, Plunket and other health professionals involved with this baby in future have access to that information

Fluids and Nutrition
Simple facts:
There are differences in the preferred fluid types and volumes in different neonatal and Special Care Baby units
There is evidence that restricting the volume in the first few days in premature infants can reduce the risk of NEC and PDA
Weight change  is the gold standard in assessing hydration
It is expected for newborn babies to lose up to 10% of their BW in the first few days of life
Expected urine output is 1 wet nappy in the first 24hrs, 2 in the 2nd and over 4 from day 3 of life
Measured intake, urine output, blood pressure and clinical appearance are also useful
Urinary sodium and osmolality can be of value but is unreliable in patients on diuretics or inotropes
Overhead heaters and phototherapy lights can increase insensible losses
Fluid volume: 
The usual total fluid intake (TFI)  volumes are as follows :




As per ESPGHAN/ESPEN/ ESPR, the recommended range of TFI for the first few days of life depends on GA and BW: 
















The calculations should be based on whichever is greater : birth weight or current weight 
In  a term baby:
 enteral feeds can be started initially at 40ml/kg/day ; 
IV fluids are usually started at 60ml/kg/day
In a premature infants <1500g it is standard practice to commence fluids at 80mls/kg/day
Increase TFI by 15 ml/kg/day daily 
Fluid increase should be individualized based on clinical context,  fluid balance, renal function, electrolyte status
The recommended TFI on full feeds is 140-180ml/kg depending on the gestation, BW and growth rate
The decision to exceed 165ml/kg should be at SMO discretion
The initial fluid restriction is due to:
Natural 5-7% excess of extracellular fluid  and sodium at birth in both term and preterm babies which can be lost without activating compensatory mechanisms or signs of dehydration
Creatinine clearance is low initially and increases with post natal age

Babies started on therapeutic hypothermia should be commenced on IV Dextrose 10% at 40ml/kg/day
The volume should be adjusted to avoid hypo-or hypernatraemia – see page 19 for further details
Hypernatraemia in the first few days of life is more likely to be due to dehydration than elevated total body sodium 
Hyponatraemia usually indicates fluid overload

Nutritional requirements 


Gastric aspirates – see also chapter on feed intolerance and NEC 
Routine NGT aspiration is NOT required and is not supported by the literature as a tool in assessing feeding intolerance
It increases the risk of interruption to enteral feeds
Increases time to full enteral feeds
Increases time to regain birth weight
omitting routine aspiration has not shown to increase or delay diagnosis of NEC 
Discarding gastric aspirates results in loss of essential gastric enzymes and hydrochloric acid
Gastric aspiration is an unreliable indicator of gastric tube placement – it is therefore important to measure the feeding tube’s position at the start of a shift and at every feed. Aspirating 0.1-0.5mls once per shift to check pH is recommended.
Gastric residual aspiration and evaluation is only indicated if there are other symptoms or feed intolerance or GI pathology including :
Vomiting
Abdominal distension
Abdominal wall discoloration
Blood in stool
Increased events of prematurity
Sepsis
Bilious vomiting in term or near term infants are always pathological until proven otherwise and require urgent surgical review
Assessment of aspirates :

Isolated light-moderate green or yellow residuals (Milk, lemon, mustard, wasabi, lime)  are NOT of concern
Studies have found no association between green residuals and NEC
Heavily bile stained, dark green aspirates (Avocado – spinach)  aspirates OR vomitus may indicate an intestinal pathology- feeds should be stopped pending further assessment
Haemorrhagic residuals may be consistent with NEC- feeds should be stopped pending further examination and investigations 
Management of gastric residuals :
Aspirate volume is <50% of previous 6hr feed volume, not heavily bile stained and abdominal exam is reassuring – return aspirate and continue feed
Aspirate volume is >=50% of previous 6hr feed volume ( so 2 feeds if on 3 hrly feeds) OR heavily bile stained – return aspirate, stop feed and assess the infant for abdominal pathology
If problem with residual volume persists and baby in clinically well – consider slow bolus feeds.
If the gastric residual volumes persists despite slower feeds, consider decreasing the feeds to the last well-tolerated feed volume.
Indications to withhold feeds:
Abdominal pathology:
Suspected or proven NEC
Significant abdominal distension and discoloration
Other suspected or proven abdominal pathology
Heavily bile stained OR large gastric residuals AND non-reassuring abdominal examination or clinical instability 




Feed fortification, additives, supplements and other commonly prescribed medication 
HMF (Human Milk Fortifier)
Even though breastmilk has many advantages for preterm and sick neonates, it does not meet the nutritional requirements for VLBW infants for energy, protein, calcium, phosphorus, trace elements and vitamins
HMF provides calories, protein, calcium phosphate, vitamins and electrolytes
The whey protein component is hydrolysed to break this down to smaller components
Supplementation with individual additives including phosphate, iron and sodium chloride increases osmolality to a much greater extent than addition of HMF 
Indications for HMF:
GA <32/40  OR
BW <1800g
Poor weight gain outside the above criteria
Contraindications / precautions:
Suspected or proven NEC 
(including significant abdominal distension with discolouration, blood in stool, heavily bile stained aspirates)
Significant vomiting
Sepsis
MAKE SURE ANY PHOSPHATE SUPPLEMENTS ARE STOPPED WHEN ON HMF 
When to start:
Half strength (1 sach in 50mls EBM)  when on 120ml/kg/day 
Full strength ( 1 sach in 25mls EBM) when reached 140-150mls/kg/day
When to stop:
Continue until preparing for discharge to maximise the vitamins, iron, fat, carbohydrate and protein intake and stores 
If excessive weight gain, consider reducing TFI first 
Beneprotein 
Occasionally babies born at <32/40 will be transferred from NICU on Beneprotein as an additional protein supplement once on full feeds
It is commenced if blood urea is <3.6mmol/L and growth is inadequate ( started at 0.5g/kg/day) 
Beneprotein can be added to unfortified or fortified beast milk
Dietician should be involved and a  plan from NICU dietician should be included in NICU discharge paperwork (can contact NICU if not available)
Beneprotein should be continued until growth is satisfactory , in particular head growth and length 
If excessive weight gain, consider reducing TFI first
Monitor urea weekly and adjust Beneprotein as below:






Infloran (Probiotics) 
Probiotics have been shown to reduce risk of NEC and all case mortality in premature and IUGR infants
Infloran contains Bifidobacterium bifidum and Lactobacillus acidophilus
Indications:
GA <32/40 OR BW <1800g

Day one of life irrespective of feeds

Iron supplements = ferrous sulphate
Iron is essential for brain development
Iron prophylaxis:
Indications for iron prophylaxis :
GA <37/40 OR BW < 2500g on unfortified EBM or standard formula feeds
Iron supplementation is NOT required if:
the baby is on >100ml/kg/day of FM85 fortifier or Preterm (LBW) formula
blood transfusion within last 2 weeks
multiple transfusions and increased serum ferritin level (>350mcg/L) – monitor weekly and star iron once below 350mc/L
When to start:
Commence at 2 weeks of age if on full feeds or on discharge, whichever occurs earliest
Prophylactic dose:
3mg/kg elemental iron PO daily (0.5ml/kg/day) 
Dose does not require adjustment for weight after discharge (usual dose on discharge 1-2ml/day) 
When to stop:
Continue for 6-12 months (depending on diet)
On discharge, ensure that “child –proof closure” is specified on the discharge prescription and advise parents of the potential from accidental poisoning
Treatment of iron deficiency anaemia:
Increase dose to 1ml/kg/day Ferrous sulphate (6mg/kg /day) OR
Start ferrous sulphate at 0.5ml/kg/day in babies receiving fully fortified EBM or LBW formula ( who wouldn’t normally need prophylaxis) 
Assess haematological response within 2-4 weeks, including ferritin
Continue treatment for 3 months after Hb recovery

Vitamin D:
Vitamin D enhances intestinal absorption of calcium and phosphorus and support a large number of physiological processes such as bone mineralisation 
Premature infants are vitamin D deficient due to lower fat stores and prolonged hospitalisation. Many women are also vitamin D deficient during pregnancy 
Vitamin D supplementation practice varies between different units in the country – the recommendations below are based on Capital and Coast and Auckland NICU recommendations 
Vitamin D prophylaxis
Indications for Vit D prophylaxis 
Fat malabsorption
ALL exclusively or partially breastfed babies
Vitamin D prophylaxis is NOT routinely needed if :
On full formula feeds
When to start:
By 4 weeks of age
Dose 
Clinicians vitamin D 1 drop daily (= 400IU cholecalciferol) if term
Clinicians vitamin D 2 drops daily (=800IU cholecalciferol) if GA <37/40 OR BW <2500g
  When to stop:
Continue for 12 months

Vitamin D deficiency (serum 25 hydroxyvitamin D <50nmol/L) with normal Ca/PO4/ALP
Double current dose (ie. 4 drops if GA <37/40, or 2 drops if term)
Recheck Vit D status in 2 weeks
Continue for 2-3 months (with monthly monitoring of Ca,PO4, ALP, 25-OH Vit D)
Calcitriol
Active form of Vitamin D
It may be considered instead of Clinicians vitamin D in neonates with Vit D deficiency AND significant liver or renal impairment if adequate Vit D levels not achieved with Cholecalciferol 
Dose : 0.1microgram/kg/day
Requires serum Ca, phosphate and PTH monitoring 
Phosphate and Calcium
All neonates born with BW <1800g should receive phosphate and calcium supplementation either as:
HMF or
Adding both calcium and phosphate to EBM starting at 1mmol/kg/day in 2-4 divided doses and adjusting according to serum levels – be aware of high osmolality 
Calcium and phosphate supplements should NOT be given together in the same feed if supplemented separately
Most infants will have this discontinued prior to discharge
Sodium supplements:
Sodium deficiency is associated with neurological sequelae and poor growth
Urinary sodium content may be helpful in a premature neonate with poor growth and a serum sodium <136 mmol/L to ascertain the total body stores of sodium
Aim for a urinary sodium > 30 mmol/L if poor growth.
Recommended target range for serum sodium is 135-145mmol/L
Sodium requirements:
Sodium is generally started at 1-2mmol/kg/day
Extreme and very preterm neonates have increased sodium losses, and a minimum intake of 6 to 8 mmol/kg/day of sodium is recommended. Some may require an intake as high as 12 mmol/kg/day.

Consider respiratory support, clinical state, events, oxygen saturation histogram prior to stopping caffeine 
Consider stopping at 35 weeks if born at gestation >28 weeks
Consider stopping at 36 weeks if born at gestation <28 weeks

Criteria for stopping
No apnoea or events requiring stimulation for 5 days
No or infrequent self correcting desaturations/bradycardias for 3 days
Max one event per hour
< 3 events per day
Histogram saturations <5% of time with sats <90%
Assessment post stopping
Review histogram regularly for (ideally) 7 days following stopping caffeine, aiming for <5% of time <90%
If no concerns 5 days following stopping caffeine could be discharged

Nutritional support in babies with conjugated jaundice
Consider early referral to dietician
Malabsorption of long chain fatty acids and fat soluble vitamins is common

Optimus Vitamin A drops. 6 drops (0.18mL) daily PO

Meconium Aspiration Syndrome (MAS)
Meconium stained amniotic fluid (MSAF) comes in various consistencies – thin, moderate and thick. 
Thin occurs in 50-60% of MSAF deliveries and carries a 2-3% risk of needing some respiratory assistance, with 1% needing ventilation. 
Thick occurs in 15% and 25-30% require significant resuscitation and 10% on going respiratory support 
8-12% of deliveries are complicated by meconium stained liquor. 
In a study by Wiswell 2000, 7% developed respiratory distress of which 3% had MAS and 4% other disorders.
Risk Factors for Meconium Aspiration Syndrome 
Primiparity 
Pacific Island ethnicity 
Male gender 
Fetal distress from hypoxia / infection etc (usual trigger for in-utero meconium passage)
Low cord pH 
Abnormal CTG (if severe) 
Thick meconium. 
Management of meconium stained in the liquor 
The Paediatric RMO should be called if there is thick meconium staining or light meconium plus fetal distress
Cord gases should be sent (please ensure this is done)
There is no advantage in oral and pharyngeal suction as the head delivers and this is not indicated. 
If an experienced intubator is present and the baby is apnoeic and hasn’t cried after cord is cut and the baby is delivered to the resuscitaire consider tracheal suction with the meconium aspirator. This should not significantly delay resuscitation efforts. Evidence for this is low level.
Nasal and oropharyngeal (under direct vision) suctioning should be pursued if obvious meconium present and/or obstructing
Normal resuscitation guidelines should be followed
All infants with meconium stained liquor need meconium obs (minimum 4hrly) and should not be discharged within 24hours.
Consider planned early (1-2hour) RMO review in infants with significant meconium exposure
Admit to SCBU if :
Symptomatic meconium aspiration syndrome
High risk for HIE
Other significant risks for infection
Management in SCBU:
Give enough oxygen to maintain SpO2 >95% initially (to avoid PPHN). 
Put on CPAP to deliver oxygen. 
CXR if severe respiratory distress, sudden deterioration or tachypnoea persists >4hrs. At high risk of air leaks.
Consider antibiotics (refer sepsis guideline): 
Take blood culture if treating with antibiotics. 
Monitor for complications of meconium aspiration:
Persistent Pulmonary Hypertension: 
Is common in severe MAS and can be very difficult to treat. 
Early assessment and avoidance of hypoxaemia, hypothermia, hypoglycaemia are important to avert PPHN
Pneumothorax: 
May occur at any stage (MAS is an air-trapping disease). 
Consider this any time from resuscitation onwards if there is unexpected deterioration or significant disease. 
Respiratory Failure: 
Can occur because of respiratory obstruction, inflammation, infection or shunting. 
Infection 
Uncommon, unless sepsis was the stimulus to make the infant pass meconium
Consider Listeria in preterm
Meconium is a good culture medium so secondary infection may occur

Coarse infiltrates

PPHN is characterised by persistent fetal circulation, high pulmonary pressures, right to left shunting, severe hypoxia which can be unresponsive to oxygen therapy and confers a high mortality / morbidity. It can be challenging to differentiate this from primary critical congenital cardiac defects.
PPHN can be
Primary
Usually idiopathic
Secondary to primary dysfunction of the pulmonary epithelium
Secondary (more common)
Birth asphyxia
MAS
Diaphragmatic hernia
Sepsis
Severe RDS
Hypoplastic lungs
Polycythaemia 
Maternal SSRI use (plus some other medications)
Acidosis and circulatory failure can cause and worsen PPHN
Neonates with PPHN are often very fragile and difficult to manage
Diagnosis :
This is based on excluding cyanotic congenital heart disease
Assess the difference in pre and post ductal oxygen levels (measure in right hand and a foot); >3% difference is significant
Diagnosis is confirmed on ECHO
Management :
Goals of management;
Lower pulmonary vascular resistance
Maintain systemic blood pressure
Reverse right to left shunting
Improve oxygenation 
Minimise barotrauma
Management :
These infants will be urgently transferred to NICU, involve SMO
Oxygenation and ventilation;
100% oxygen
Intubate and ventilate ( HFOV is most effective)
Volume expansion (10ml/kg bolus saline) to maintain normotension

Cardiac murmurs 
https://www.clinicalguidelines.scot.nhs.uk/ggc-paediatric-guidelines/ggc-guidelines/neonatology/heart-murmurs-in-the-neonate-an-approach-to-the-neonate-with-a-heart-murmur/
Simple facts :
Many babies will have an audible murmur in the first 48-72 hrs of life
This is caused by constriction of the ductus arteriosus or other circulatory changes related to the perinatal transition
The murmur of normal ductal closure in a well term infant is typically an ejection systolic murmur at the left sternal edge. This is generally caused by increased flow through the pulmonary valve from LR shunting through the duct. It can be reasonably loud but should never be accompanied by other abnormal cardiac signs such as cyanosis, abnormal femoral pulses, thrill or significant tachycardia / tachypnoea.
Only small minority of the murmurs will indicate a congenital heart defect but these are essential to recognise.
In excluding serious congenital heart disease, the rest of the cardiovascular examination is just as important as auscultating the heart. 
A neonate with any of the following findings needs urgent assessment even if a murmur is not present:
Signs of heart failure of shock
Lower limb saturations <96% in the absence of respiratory disease
>3% difference between pre and post ductal saturations
Absent / weak femoral pulses

 What to do if you hear a murmur on postnatal ward in a well baby 
Well baby with normal examination otherwise and normal saturation screening:
Perform full cardiovascular examination assessing for:
Dysmorphic features 
Signs of heart failure (tachypnoea, increased respiratory effort, hepatomegaly, shock)
Palpation of brachial and femoral pulses
Presence of cyanosis (as measured by lower limb saturations – a reading < 96% or >3% difference between pre and post ductal saturations should prompt further investigation 
Heart sounds
Presence of a heave
Murmur – intensity, character, location and radiation
Pre and post ductal saturations 
Review by registrar (or consultant if high level of suspicion for a congenital heart disease) 
All infants with heart murmur should remain in hospital until >24hrs of age
Re-examine the baby daily until murmur resolved or baby is discharged. 
If the baby remains well, pre and post ductal saturations are normal but the murmur persists on discharge :
Explain and give parents the written information leaflet describing warning signs and advising what to do if baby became unwell
Ensure follow up in RMO clinic within 6 weeks
Additional investigations :
ECG
Not routinely performed as has been shown not aid significantly in the diagnosis other than AVSD ( more common in Trisomy 21)
CXR and 4 limb BPs
There is no evidence to support the use of CXR or 4 limb BP in the assessment of neonates with heart murmurs 
ECHO
Likely significant congenital heart disease requiring same day ECHO  (will need transfer to NICU) is  indicated if cardiac murmur AND any of the below :
Persistent lower limb saturations <96%
Positive congenital cardiac screening ( >3% pre/post ductal saturation difference)
Absent / weak femoral pulses
Heart failure
Shock
Urgent inpatient or outpatient ECHO  is indicated in an asymptomatic baby with a pathological murmur :
Dysmorphism
Heave
Abnormal heart sounds
Loud murmur (>2/6)
Pan systolic, diastolic or continuous murmur
Location other than left sternal edge/radiation
Asymptomatic baby with low risk of congenital heart disease requires a routine review in RMO  clinic in 2-6 weeks and consideration of ECHO if murmur persists:
Well infants 
No signs of heart failure,
Normal pulses

Supraventricular Tachycardia 
Simple facts:
30-40% of SVT presents in the first few weeks after birth but can occur antenatally. 
The majority of SVT in neonates is due to a re-entrant rhythm (extra electrical connection) 
Presentation 
Often asymptomatic and tolerated for many hours

Management 
Non-Pharmacological Management 
Only use this in patient who are haemodynamically stable and not in shock 
Explain the procedure to the parents 
Cardiorespiratory monitoring in place 
Wrap arms in a towel and immerse the whole face (including mouth and nose – no need to occlude these as the baby will hold its breath) in an ice water slurry in a bowl/bucket for 5 seconds  
If unable to dip baby into the water place an ice cold facecloth to the face for 10 seconds

IV access in as large a vein as possible – antecubital fossa is preferred; place a 3-way tap 
Adenosine rapid bolus through one port then a rapid bolus of saline flush up to 5mL 
Adenosine temporarily blocks the AV node so asystole will be seen briefly before reverting to sinus rhythm 
Dose at 100mcg/kg then 200mcg/kg then 300mcg/kg and if still unsuccessful:
Check technique:
Rapid bolus of adenosine followed immediately by rapid bolus of NS into a large vein, preferably in ACF

Record the rhythm strip when giving adenosine

Take a 12 lead ECG once back in sinus rhythm 
Echo
Half of the babies will have recurrence of SVT in infancy but 90% will have stopped by 1 year of age

Simple facts:
Polycythaemia  affects up to 5% of healthy term newborns
It is defined as a venous haematocrit that greatly exceeds normal values for gestational and postnatal age
Term baby : Hct >65%

Neonatal Sepsis – General Facts

Neonatal sepsis can be devastating if not treated promptly - the baby can appear relatively stable until rapid decompensation occurs
The younger the infant, the more nonspecific presentation 
Early GBS sepsis:
Over 80% of early GBS sepsis occurs in infants born to mother with negative swabs 
Over 90% cases are clinically apparent within the first 24hrs of life
Clinical presentations include :
Generalized sepsis without a focus – 80-85%
Congenital pneumonia – 10%
Meningitis – 7%
Late onset GBS sepsis:
Clinical presentations usually include:

Meningitis – 25-30%

Neurology 
Hypoxic Ischaemic Encephalopathy
Following a reversible hypoxic ischaemic global insult, neuronal death occurs in two major phases:
Primary neuronal loss related to cellular hypoxia – there may be immediate neuronal death if the insult is severe , however, many neurons do not die during this primary phase of injury
Secondary neuronal loss and delayed neuronal death starts hours after the initial insult and extends over days. This phase is associated with increased seizure activity and marked encephalopathy.s
There is a window of opportunity that exists in the interval following resuscitation of the asphyxiated newborn before the secondary phase – this has been shown to be of 6 hours
Borderline cases not entirely meeting criteria which can be discussed with NICU include :
Well documented history of acute intrapartum asphyxia, but no or only mild encephalopathy 
Baby meeting criteria other than age ( 6-24hrs old)

Stage 1:	hyper alertness, hyperreflexia, dilated pupils, tachycardia, seizures uncommon

Neonatal Seizures
Summary
Prompt discussion with the on-call SMO is mandatory for all infants suspected to be having seizures or who are at risk for newborn seizures. 
If there is concern for seizures, the most helpful thing you can do is ensure that the events are captured on video
Clinical diagnosis of seizures in this age group may be challenging. Focal tonic and focal clonic motor events are the most convincing for a neonatal seizure. A wide range of other clinical presentations may be seen, however. 
First line treatment of newborn seizures is usually phenobarbitone; however, levetiracetam, phenytoin or midazolam may also be used (and occasionally carbamazepine). Treatment is to be on the advice of the on-call SMO. 

Background
Seizures are the most common neurological emergency occurring in the newborn period (defined as the first 28 days or before 44 weeks CGA in a preterm infant). 
Seizures may occur in 14.28 infants/1000 live births in preterm babies vs 1.1 infants/1000 live births in term infants
Hypoxic-ischaemic encephalopathy is the single most common cause of seizures; however, vascular events, structural brain lesions, infections, transient metabolic disturbances, inborn errors of metabolism and genetic epilepsies are also seen (see figure)
Most newborn seizures are provoked by acute illness states and so are not due to epilepsy. Approximately 5-10% of newborns with seizures have a neonatal epilepsy (i.e. recurrent unprovoked seizures). Neonatal epilepsies often require specific investigation and treatment and early discussion with a neurologist is recommended. 


Figure 1: Relative frequencies of the usual causes of newborn seizures

Presentation
Seizures may present as motor events (e.g. tonic, clonic, myoclonic, spasms, automatisms), nonmotor events (e.g. autonomic derangement, apnoea, behavior arrest), sequential events (i.e. displaying a range of different sequential clinical signs without a dominant clinical feature) or may be unclassifiable. 
Seizures may also occur in infants with no observable clinical change, these seizures will only be detected by EEG/aEEG monitoring in the tertiary centre and are termed electrographic-only seizures
Of note, all seizures in newborns are focal onset. 
The table below contains descriptions of the seizure types that may be seen in newborn babies. 


Diagnosis
Diagnosis is extremely challenging as clinical events corresponding to seizures may be subtle or non-existent (i.e. electrographic-only seizures). Even experienced clinicians may struggle to correctly diagnose seizures on clinical grounds alone. 
EEG or aEEG electrographic monitoring is usually required for accurate diagnosis and monitoring for newborns where there is a high suspicion of newborn seizures. This would require transfer to NICU, so early discussion with the on-call local SMO is important in case transfer is needed.
Focal tonic or focal clonic motor events are highly likely to represent seizures, these events may be classified as seizures (and treatment considered) on clinical grounds. 
If an event can be reliably clinically provoked by stimulation of the infant, or restraint of infant limbs during spontaneous events prompts an arrest of the events, then the event is likely not a seizure.
Neonatal sleep myoclonus is a common seizure mimic in this age group. It only occurs during sleep and consists of brief focal myoclonic movements of the limbs. Classically flows from one limb to another during sleep. It never affects the face. The events stop when the infant is woken. This condition is benign and requires no treatment. Video may help if there is uncertainty.
The figure and table below demonstrates the recommended approach to diagnosing seizures in newborn babies, including where transfer to NICU for EEG/aEEG monitoring should be considered. Don’t worry about the definition of an EEG/aEEG correlate as the EEG/aEEG is interpreted by the NICU team after transfer. 
Any clinical diagnosis of a neonatal seizure should be discussed with the on-call SMO.

Ammonia ( consider full metabolic screen) 
Consider HSV PCR

Viral PCR as indicated
Consider sending an extra tube of CSF for freezing especially if infectious or metabolic cause is suspected

MRI often will be needed but unlikely to be available acutely. 
Additional genetic and metabolic investigations may also be required.  
Treatment
Resuscitation: ABCs. Respiratory depression or frequent apnoea may necessitate intubation and ventilation. 
Treat any underlying causes: Consider antibiotics, consider acyclovir if HSV suspected, correct hypoglycaemia or electrolyte derangement. Hyperammonaemia/organic acidaemia/other metabolic derangement may require specific treatment on specialist advice. 
Antiseizure medications may be required after discussion with the on-call SMO. Phenobarbitone is the usual first-line choice. Levetiracetam, midazolam, phenytoin and occasionally carbamazepine may also be used depending on specialist advice. The Australasian neonatal medicines formulary contains doses and drug information https://www.anmfonline.org/ 
Remember that HIE is the most common cause of newborn seizures. These babies will often also benefit from therapeutic hypothermia and this consideration should also be discussed with the on-call SMO.

Metabolic / Endocrine 

Hypoglycaemia 
Definition:
There is no clear consensus as to what threshold to use in treatment of hypoglycaemia – in Australia and NZ the most common cut off for treatment is <2.6mmol/L
Background:
Healthy term babies are able to mobilise energy stores through a process known as counter regulation and are unlikely to suffer any ill effects if fed infrequently in the first 48hrs of life. Some babies however are less able to cope and are at great risk for hypoglycaemia
Even asymptomatic hypoglycaemia may result in adverse outcome (cerebral injury, neurodevelopmental delay).

Maternal beta blockers are a significant risk factor for hypoglycaemia and bradycardia in newborn infants. 
Infants born to mothers taking a beta blocker in the third trimester for whatever reason should have initial management and BSL monitoring similar to other high risk infants (summarised below) AND 24 hours of 4 hrly obs including HR to identify significant bradycardia.

This risk factor should be recording on the NOC NEWS chart highlighting the above plan.

Suggested management
-Offer uninterrupted skin-to-skin contact at birth, or as soon as able following birth
-Promote early breastfeeding within the first hour of life and assess for effective breastfeed
-If breastfeeding is not effective, then help the mother to express her colostrum, and give all available expressed breast milk (EBM) to the infant but not beyond guidance volume
-If no EBM is available, a formula feed should be offered at 5ml/kg/feed three hourly 
- Check the BSL of an infant at risk of hypoglycaemia one hour after the first feed (at a maximum of 2 hours of age)
-Ensure regular three hourly feeds, or earlier if showing feeding cues
-Maintain infant temperature (skin to skin where appropriate) between 36.5°C and 37.5°C and assess responsiveness
-Continue pre-feed BSLs for the next three feeds
-There must be three consecutive pre-feed results ≥ 2.6 mmol/L before BSLs may be discontinued. Note: If the pre-feed BSLs are showing a downward trend, then monitoring should continue
- If an infant has symptoms of hypoglycaemia at any time, perform BSL immediately
- Notify the neonatal medical staff if the result is < 2.6mmol/L and follow the management plan as per hypoglycaemia guideline

Maternal thyroid disease –Guideline from Waikato Neonatal Guidelines 2024
Maternal Hyperthyroidism
Hyperthyroidism affects 0.1-0.4% of pregnancies and the most common aetiologies are autoimmune hyperthyroidism (Graves’ disease) (95%), and gestational transient hyperthyroidism (1-3%)

Neonatal autoimmune hyperthyroidism (neonatal Graves’ disease) affects about 2% of infants of mothers who have autoimmune hyperthyroidism (Graves’ disease) (Ref 1). It is caused by TSH receptor antibodies that have crossed the placenta leading to clinical
symptoms in the foetus and neonate. It can occur in babies of mothers with active disease or those who have been treated in the past but still have circulating antibodies.

It is important to remember that mothers who have had a past history of autoimmune hyperthyroidism can become hypothyroid following treatment, and then require thyroxine. These mothers can still have circulating antithyroid antibodies. Infants of these mothers
are also at risk of neonatal autoimmune hyperthyroidism.
Features of fetal and neonatal hyperthyroidism
Foetus
Features in the foetus include goitre, FGR, oligohydramnios, tachycardia, hydrops (associated with heart failure), increased risk of fetal compromise.

Neonate
In the neonate symptoms can present in the first few days and may last 3-6 months proportional to the clearance of maternal transplacentally acquired antibodies. There can be delayed presentation in babies of treated mothers due to the effects of transplacentally
transferred maternal antithyroid drugs or blocking antibodies. Neonatal features can include goitre, irritability, restlessness, excessive weight loss, failure to thrive, diarrhoea, sweating, flushing, eye signs (peri-orbital oedema, lid retraction, proptosis), tachycardia,
cardiac failure, advanced bone age, craniosynostosis, microcephaly, hepatosplenomegaly, thrombocytopenia, hyperviscosity.

When assessing a neonate it is important to appreciate that these features can be nonspecific and a range of differential diagnoses need to be considered including sepsis, hypoglycaemia, drug withdrawal, and other causes for cardiorespiratory compromise.

Maternal Hypothyroidism
The most common cause of primary hypothyroidism in reproductive age is chronic autoimmune hypothyroidism (Hashimoto’s thyroiditis). There may be circulating stimulating or inhibiting antibodies that may transiently affect the neonate. There is a very small risk of hypothyroidism in the baby and this will be picked up by the routine newborn metabolic screen (NST – national screening test).

There are significant alterations in thyroid function during pregnancy and increased dietary iodine requirements. Iodine, TRH, antithyroid medications and most maternal thyroid hormones, and IgG antibodies cross the placenta and yet there is no transplacental
passage of TSH (Ref 4). Levothyroxine therapy may be required for treatment of maternal hypothyroidism and this crosses the placenta protecting the foetus from hypothyroidism until postpartum (Ref 4). It is of course essential to optimise the maternal management in pregnancy to prevent any risk to the foetus of hypothyroidism.

Physiological Changes in the Newborn
There is a natural physiological surge in TSH peaking at about 30 minutes after birth and then reducing to normal term levels by 3-5 days. The TSH surge stimulates neonatal T4 production which peaks by 48 hours after birth with normalisation of T4 (free) levels over
several weeks (Ref 6). For this reason it is important to interpret any thyroid function results in context of the clinical history and age of the baby. For example a marginal raised T4 (free) in a 5 day old asymptomatic baby may reflect the physiological changes in T4 (free) rather than neonatal hyperthyroidism. The optimal timing of thyroid function tests in the newborn is debatable.

History
It is very helpful to have a detailed maternal history.
Specific to the maternal thyroid history it is valuable to have the following information:

Maternal diagnosis
Graves’ disease (Autoimmune hyperthyroidism) YES or NO
Hashimoto’s Thyroiditis (Autoimmune hypothyroidism) YES or NO
Other maternal thyroid disorders e.g.….aplasia, ectopic, post thyroidectomy for Ca,
genetic disorders

Maternal Treatment
Anti-thyroid drugs
Thyroxine
Past thyroid surgery

Maternal TSI antibody levels
Note maternal TSI antibody values
Plan for management or the baby should be documented during the antenatal period
One of the most common scenarios is that of a mother who has been on thyroxine but the specific diagnosis and TSI antibody status is unknown. This makes it difficult to assess the risk to the infant and determine the need for any further investigations. For such infants,
consideration should be given to test cord blood for TSI antibodies.
For infants of mothers with Graves’ disease Request TSH, T3 (free), T4 (free) on Day 5-7 

If the mother has been on antithyroid medication arrange for baby to have repeat TFTs (T4 (free), TSH) on Day 10-14.

A follow-up clinical review by the LMC at 7-14 days. This is because there can be a delayed presentation of hyperthyroidism in this clinical scenario.

If there is a need to test for TSH Receptor Antibodies or TSI Thyroid-Stimulating Immunoglobulin (Grave’s disease specific), please also send a serum sample

For all other thyroid problems (including maternal hypothyroidism) no routine TFTs required, but ensure newborn screening testing is sent.

If results suggest hyperthyroidism, then consult with neonatal SMO and consider Paediatric Endocrinology opinion regarding further management.

a) If baby asymptomatic, the results will gradually normalise over a few months and can be monitored at a frequency decided by the responsible clinician. A suggested approach is fortnightly TFTs for a month and then monthly until levels normalise.

b) If baby is symptomatic, then recommend early consult with Paediatric Endocrinology as baby may require antithyroid medications if symptoms are moderate to severe.
Infants with mild symptoms can still be monitored with serial TFTs as per the asymptomatic infant above. Treatment with antithyroid drugs is rarely needed as symptoms improve as the transplacentally acquired antibody levels reduce.

It is recommended that if baby has clinical signs of hyperthyroidism, consult with NICU regarding management. The neonatal team can determine whether Paediatric Endocrine opinion is required.

If any clinical signs of hyperthyroidism (see section 2.2 Features of fetal and neonatal hyperthyroidism) consider early TFTs (T4 [free], TSH) with understanding that there is usually a TSH surge shortly after delivery and results should be interpreted with consideration for the age of the baby.

Toxic erythema of newborn – Erythema Toxicum Neonatorum













Affects at least 50% of newborn babies
Arises in the first few days and presents with scattered pink or red marks often with papules and wheals scattered over the whole body
It is benign and resolves after a few days while new lesions appear

Transient pustular melanosis






Also known as transient neonatal pustular dermatosis and transient neonatal pustulosis 
It may a variant  of toxic erythema of newborn 
It affects 0.2-4% of newborn infants in the first few days of life
It is more common in dark skinned babies 
It presents with pustules on an unaffected, non erythematous base present at birth
It can involve any area, including palms and soles
Lesions can be solitary or occur in clusters
The pustules can rupture and crust
The lesions can leave an area of hyperpigmentation which fades over the next few months
