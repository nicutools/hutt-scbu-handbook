Diagnosis
-

Diagnosis is extremely challenging as clinical events corresponding to seizures may be subtle or nonexistent (i.e. electrographic-only seizures). Even experienced clinicians may struggle to correctly diagnose
seizures on clinical grounds alone.

-

EEG or aEEG electrographic monitoring is usually required for accurate diagnosis and monitoring for
newborns where there is a high suspicion of newborn seizures. This would require transfer to NICU, so
early discussion with the on-call local SMO is important in case transfer is needed.

-

Focal tonic or focal clonic motor events are highly likely to represent seizures, these events may be
classi ed as seizures (and treatment considered) on clinical grounds.

-

If an event can be reliably clinically provoked by s mula on of the infant, or restraint of infant limbs
during spontaneous events prompts an arrest of the events, then the event is likely not a seizure.

-

Neonatal sleep myoclonus is a common seizure mimic in this age group. It only occurs during sleep and
consists of brief focal myoclonic movements of the limbs. Classically ows from one limb to another
during sleep. It never a ects the face. The events stop when the infant is woken. This condi on is benign
and requires no treatment. Video may help if there is uncertainty.
